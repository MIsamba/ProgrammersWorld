When hearing the word computational thinking, the first thing that comes up to your mind is that, it is thinking like a computer. Infact some might say it is all about programming. Some might even say, it is all about how the computer thinks. But the truth is that a computer does not think, it only does what you instruct it to do.

Before we dive into what computational thinking is, let's clear some misconceptions first.

computational thinking is not
  • Thinking like a computer.
  • How the computer thinks.
  • Programming.
Now read carefully

Computational thinking is simply the process of following the 4 stages (or procedures), to develop a solution for a real life (or complex) problem, in a way that a computer, human or both can understand.

The process in which you have a complex problem, understand it and then develop a solution for it by following the 4 stages (or procedures), is termed computational thinking.

To summarize it all, computational thinking is just all about solving problems by following the 4 stages (or procedures) involved.

The keywords here to remember are
  1. Developing a solution for a problem,
  2. by following the 4 stages (or procedures),
  3. in a way that a computer, human or both can understand.
The 4 stages (or procedures) to follow when talking about computational thinking are
  1. Decomposition; is simply the process of breaking down bigger (or complex) problem into smaller more manageable ones.
  2. Pattern Recognition; is simply the process of recognizing a successful pattern used in solving a similar problem, to solve your own specific problem, out of the smaller more manageable ones.
  3. Abstraction; is simply the process of filtering out and focusing on the more important problem first, out of the smaller more manageable ones.
  4. Algorithm (or Algorithmic thinking); is simply the process of writing (or sketching) down set of instructions to be followed when solving the smaller more manageable problems.

As you can see, the 4 stages of computational thinking are like the 4 legs of a table, if one leg is missing, it becomes incomplete.
